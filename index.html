The challenge

AI inference is an energy crisis

Training a large language model emits as much CO₂ as a transatlantic flight. But the real scale problem is inference — the billions of daily queries that run on racks of power-hungry GPUs, each consuming hundreds of watts continuously.

The dominant operation in neural networks is multiplying an input vector by a weight matrix. On silicon transistors this means fetching gigabytes of weight data and toggling billions of transistors every second. Physics sets a hard floor on how efficiently electrons can do this.

Photons do not have that floor. Light encodes, routes, and multiplies coherently with near-zero marginal energy per operation. Once the weights are set, the optical field propagates and computes for free.

Core technology

Three key innovations

Each pillar of our architecture solves a different bottleneck that has held photonic computing back — together they make fully on-chip AI inference possible.

〜

On-Chip Non-Linear Activation

Industry first

Competitors such as Lightelligence perform matrix multiplication optically but are forced to execute activation functions on a separate electronic chip — every layer boundary requires a digital-to-analogue conversion that burns energy and adds latency.

Loop Labs eliminates these "data hops" entirely. We implement activation functions directly on the photonic chip using homodyne detectors that perform the non-linearity in the analogue optical domain — no round-trip to electronics required.

🔷

Lithium Niobate Platform

Best-in-class material

Ultra-low optical loss (~3 dB/m) and sub-picojoule modulation — once a weight is set via the Pockels effect, the chip holds it capacitively at near-zero static power, while GPU DRAM spends 10–100 pJ just to fetch that same weight.

Validated by the ZEN-1 prototype: 33.5 fJ/OP energy efficiency, 0.64 TOPS throughput, 98.5% matrix fidelity, and 99% classification accuracy on non-linear datasets — the platform is proven well beyond proof of concept.

🔁

Loop Architecture

Space-to-time pivot

Traditional photonic arrays scale computation by adding more hardware in parallel — a wider chip for a deeper network. Our loop architecture shifts that axis: time replaces space. The same compact modulator mesh is reused on every 225 ps loop cycle, implementing each additional layer without any additional chip area.

The result is massive hardware miniaturisation — the same compute power as a sprawling photonic array fits in a die small enough for edge and mobile deployment.

⬡

Modular Tensor Scalability

Beyond 100 channels

Today's most advanced photonic processors top out at around 100 channels — far too few for large-scale AI. Our modular architecture connects multiple loop processors using tensor decomposition, scaling compute capacity without redesigning the core chip.

Each module remains compact and independently verifiable. Tensor decomposition discovers the optimal wiring between modules automatically, providing a clear path to the channel counts required by transformer and LLM inference.

Inverse power scaling law — unlike GPUs whose energy cost grows quadratically with model size, our TFLN architecture becomes more efficient as the network scales. The larger the model, the greater our advantage.

🔦

1 · Encode input

The neural network input is encoded as the intensity and phase of light on parallel waveguides using on-chip modulators at 1550 nm.

🔀

2 · Optical multiply

Light propagates through the modulator mesh. Each element couples power between adjacent waveguides according to its programmed weight — the matrix multiply happens as photons travel.

〜

3 · On-chip activation

Homodyne detectors apply the non-linear activation function entirely in the optical domain — no conversion to electronics, no round-trip latency.

🔁

4 · Recirculate

The activated field feeds back into the chip via the loop. Each 225 ps pass adds another network layer using the same hardware, with no extra die area.

About Loop Labs

Building the optical computer for AI

Loop Labs is an early-stage deep-tech company developing integrated photonic chips for AI inference acceleration. Our technology was born from a strategic research collaboration between the Università degli Studi di Milano and Politecnico di Milano, combining deep expertise in integrated photonics, electro-optic materials, and machine learning systems.

Our ultimate objective is to break the chain that confines advanced AI to multi-megawatt datacenters. By computing with photons instead of electrons, we aim to bring massive generative AI to edge devices — autonomous vehicles, robotics, and everyday consumer electronics — making AI ubiquitous, instantaneous, and sustainable.

Development roadmap

  • Now: Feedforward Neural Networks and MLPs — fully validated on standard non-linear datasets
  • Next: Convolutional Neural Networks and ResNets for computer vision workloads
  • Target: Large Language Models and Transformers — attention mechanisms mapped natively to optical hardware
